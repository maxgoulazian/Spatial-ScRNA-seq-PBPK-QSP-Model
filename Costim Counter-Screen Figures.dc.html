A toxicity counter-screen for costimulatory T-cell engagers

CD3-only engagers deliver signal 1 but no signal 2, so recruited T cells activate without costimulation and exhaust. Adding a costim arm restores durability — but because the engaging arm hits every CD4⁺ cell, it amplifies the same wiring that drives cytokine-release syndrome and Treg expansion. We score every candidate costim receptor on three axes to find one that amplifies the killers while sparing the CD4 suppressive and CRS programs, then rank tumor-conditional targets to carry it.

FIG 15

The window is a toxicity problem — and the model proves it

A whole-body sweep across three solid-tumor antigens × every costim arm. Tumor kill is arm-invariant (no-costim ≈ armed) — so choosing the arm is a pure toxicity-minimization problem. CD28 is the worst offender on every antigen, raising off-tumor kill +40–57% with no tumor benefit — quantitatively reproducing the TGN1412 and CEACAM gut-toxicity liabilities from expression and geometry alone. The two co-leads stay at near-baseline (+4–8%).

Whole-body PBPK/QSP off-tumor sweep · source COSTIM_OFFTOX_ANALYSIS_2026-07-14.csv (3 antigens × 12 arms). The framework doubles as an antigen×arm co-selector: CD19-heme » EGFR (TI 1.3) > CEACAM6 (0.6) > CEACAM5 (0.5). Hover any bar for exact values.
CD28 is a singular off-tumor amplifier — ~5× the next arm, with zero on-tumor benefit — reproducing the TGN1412 superagonist signature from expression alone. The activation-induced arms (including co-lead 4-1BB) sit at or below the no-costim baseline. Caveat: this is a CD28-singular effect, not a strict constitutive-vs-induced law — CD226 is constitutive yet the lowest (net-negative) arm.
