Project title: A toxicity counter-screen for costimulatory T-cell engagers: using a CD4⁺ Perturb-seq map to nominate a costim arm that amplifies effector function without feeding the CD4 suppressive and cytokine-release programs.  

Partner dataset: Marson / Pritchard genome-scale CD4⁺ T-cell CRISPRi Perturb-seq.  

The problem. CD3-only T-cell engagers deliver "signal 1" but no "signal 2," so the recruited T cell activates without costimulation, exhausts, and the response is short-lived. 

The field's answer is to add a costimulatory arm (CD28, 4-1BB), and tumor-targeted costim bispecifics are now well into clinical and preclinical development. The unsolved problem is toxicity, and it has a specific origin. Because the engaging arm is anti-CD3, present on all T cells, the molecule cannot avoid engaging CD4⁺ T cells, and adding a costim arm then amplifies whatever those CD4 cells do. Three mechanisms drive the harm, and only one is truly a CD4-subset problem. First, cytokine-release syndrome: CD4 helper cells are disproportionate cytokine producers, so broad CD4 activation seeds an IFN-γ/TNF/IL-2 cascade that myeloid cells amplify through IL-6 and IL-1, scaling with the magnitude and systemic breadth of activation. Second, regulatory-T-cell expansion: Tregs are CD4⁺ and express CD28 and 4-1BB at high levels, so a costim arm risks expanding the immunosuppressive compartment, sharpest for CD28 and for 4-1BB. Third, costim-intrinsic toxicity independent of subset: the CD28 superagonist TGN1412 caused near-fatal CRS in healthy volunteers, and 4-1BB agonists cause hepatotoxicity through 4-1BB on liver myeloid cells. The key point is that the enemy is not the CD4 lineage but a specific CD4 sub-program, the Treg-suppressive plus high-cytokine wiring. CD4 help is beneficial; it drives CD8 durability and persistence, and CD4 cells can be direct effectors, including in colorectal cancer. The field's real solutions target the sub-program, not the lineage: tumor-conditional costim, and designs that spare or deplete Tregs while expanding CD8.  

The approach. The Marson/Pritchard CD4⁺ Perturb-seq screen is a genome-scale map of exactly the programs that cause costim-engager toxicity: it resolves the regulatory / IL-10 program and scores regulators across the cytokine set that drives CRS. That makes the "wrong subset" dataset the right instrument, because CD4 is the one compartment where effector and help wiring can be separated from the suppressive and cytokine-release wiring. I will score every candidate costim receptor on three axes. Effector benefit: does agonizing it enhance CD8 effector function, read from a matched CD8 screen (primary anchor Schmidt et al. 2022, the Marson lab's own genome-wide CRISPRa/CRISPRi Perturb-seq in primary human T cells, which sorted on IFN-γ in CD8⁺ cells). This anchor is uniquely well suited because it is gain-of-function: its CRISPRa arm directly identified a set of costimulatory TNF-receptor-superfamily members (4-1BB, CD27, CD40, OX40) that promote IFN-γ when overexpressed, supplying the agonism-direction evidence that a CD4 CRISPRi knockdown screen structurally cannot. Suppression liability: does its CD4 wiring feed the IL-10 / Treg-suppressive program, read from the CD4 Perturb-seq differential-expression matrix. CRS liability: does its CD4 wiring drive the storm cytokines (TNF, IL-2, IFN-γ), read from the same matrix. The nomination is the receptor that maximizes effector benefit while minimizing both liabilities. The best case is a receptor whose agonism is CD8-effector-high and CD4-suppression/CRS-low, so the costim boost lands on the killers while sparing the CD4/Treg/CRS axis, addressing the "CD3 engages CD4 too" liability at the costim layer. Tumor-conditional delivery, the field-standard safety format, layers on top and is orthogonal to receptor choice. The three axes are, directly, the efficacy and toxicity terms of a QSP model, so this feeds my mechanistic modeling framework rather than sitting beside it.  

Deliverable. A reproducible, three-axis nomination of engager costim arms (effector benefit versus suppression and CRS liability), each scored for QSP-predicted therapeutic-window impact, with in-silico-designed binders proposed against the top nominated targets as a bench-ready next step.  

Why me. I develop antibody, bispecific, and ADC therapeutics end-to-end, from bench through preclinical proof-of-mechanism, and I build the mechanistic PK/PD/PBPK/QSP models that decide which candidates advance. This project sits exactly at that intersection. The screens supply the effector-versus-toxicity wiring; I bring the multispecific-design and QSP-modeling layer that turns a list of regulators into a buildable, dose-rationalized molecule with a predicted therapeutic window. I have already prototyped the effector/activation axis for the full costim receptor panel on the provided tables, so the approach is de-risked, not hypothetical. Almost no one in the applicant pool pairs T-cell-engager engineering with QSP translation on a single-cell screen. 

Honest scope. CD4 is the counter-screen, not the effector axis, and that is deliberate: CD8 does the killing, and the CD4 screen is used for what it uniquely resolves, the suppressive and cytokine-release programs that gate toxicity. I do not claim to measure CD8 killing in CD4 data. CRISPRi is loss-of-function while an engager arm is gain-of-function, so no screen can directly prove agonism helps; the screen supplies a validated state change and the QSP model translates it into a predicted efficacy and window. The per-receptor toxicity axes require the full deposited genome-scale regulator-by-gene matrix rather than the summary supplementary tables, and pulling and aligning that matrix is scoped as week-one work. Finally, a costim arm is subset-selective only if the receptor is differentially expressed or wired between CD8 effectors and CD4/Tregs; the screens give the functional differential and I confirm the expression differential with my RNA-to-surface-protein layer, validated experimentally on CRC antigens with QIFIKIT absolute quantification.  

One-week plan. 

(1) Effector/activation axis, already prototyped: signed scoring of the costim panel on the CD4 activation axis, yielding tiered arm candidates. 

(2) Toxicity counter-screen axes: pull the full genome-scale CD4 differential-expression matrix and score each costim receptor on the IL-10 / Treg-suppressive program and the CRS cytokine set. 

(3) CD8 effector axis: align the Marson-lab CD8 Perturb-seq (Schmidt et al. 2022\) to score effector benefit per receptor, completing the three-axis nomination. 

(4) QSP translation: feed the three axes into my scRNA-seq-integrated PBPK/QSP framework to parameterize the T-cell activation and PD arms, giving predicted killing and therapeutic-window shift, with a worked example where a Treg-aware arm beats a pan-costim arm on window. 

(5) Bench-ready next step: propose an in-silico-designed binder population against the top nominated targets (RFdiffusion, ProteinMPNN, AlphaFold with ΔΔG and confidence-metric filtering) for downstream in-vitro testing. Each rung degrades gracefully: steps 1 and 2 alone are a complete Research-track finding, a signed nomination of engager costim arms that separates effector benefit from CD4 toxicity liability.
